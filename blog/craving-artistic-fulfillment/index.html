Craving Artistic Fulfillment

Coping with the emptiness of functional design

by Kilian Kukelka
January 3, 2021 - 5 min read

Design systems

Having now spent 18 months in the professional world of web development, the initial glamour of novelty has started to fade away. What first felt like a breath of fresh air in regard to nourishing my creative needs has turned out the be a predictable process shaped by the rules of a predefined design system. Every decision about the project can be derived from this system. My thoughts have to conform to the aesthetic boundaries of the given guidelines. One may even call it declarative design. A definitive description of the desired outcome. An approach that is rock solid and inerrant once its user has mastered the tools of the trade.

The only time questions arise is when you want to extend beyond such principles. But even then, the fundamental building blocks on which you can build upon are already set. There is no room for creative destruction, nor the incentive to overthrow a working system. And why should there be? This underlying structure is a vehicle for sustaining a stable cash flow. It acts as a visual outlet for the company's values and therefore has to portray a certain level of realiability and professionality. Every notion that has the potential to obscure this image will be rejected throughout the planning stages, while future design choices will be based on objective metrics deduced from A/B-testing and key perfomance indicators. And the only way to come to peace with this objective overlord is to surrender

The price of perfection

While this logical way of translating ideas into reality has a beauty of its own, it’s practical use in the world of frontend engineering has shown to be limited when aiming for creative fullfilment. As its purpose is functional in nature, it has a hard time standing on its own. The aesthetic value of such designs always exists in relation to a product or service, with the goal being the recruitment of potential customers.

And when you spend the greater part of your week with the creation and optimization of purely functional design You Start to lose your artistic identity. While the process on its own is engaging and rewarding, I have a hard time expressing Myself through it. Design decisions are based on logic and KPIs rather than emotion and intuition. And the more Time i spend doing it, the Harder it gets to tap into my emotions. My creative intentions get lost in the now formulaic process that I’ve acquired. All I want is to be blindfolded again. Trapped in the mace of my own naivite.

Besides the joy of solving a problem there just is no emotional connection to the process itself.

Is drawing the rescue?
